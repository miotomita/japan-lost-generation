Struggles of "Lost Generation" in Japan not yet over

The assassination of Japan's former Prime Minister Shinzo Abe on July 8 has stunned the country and sparked controversy over a number of social and political issues.
Among them is rising social interest in the "Lost Generation", as the suspect in this case has been revealed to be a typical figure of the generation.



By Mio Tomita
July 21, 2022

“I feel like life is nothing more than struggling to somehow get back to zero after a negative start, ” a social media account thought to belong to Tetsuya Yamagami, 41-year-old suspect who shot the former PM, posted a few years ago.

As media coverage of the suspect's background - unemployed, financially struggling after graduating from high school in 1990s, isolated from society, and harboring grudge against society and a religious cult of which his mother was a member rather against the political figure – and posts on the social media account spread, attention began to focus on the suspect's life story.

According to Google Trends analysis, searches for "ロスジェネ(Lost Generation)" surged in Japan on the day of assassination.

Lost Generation in Japan refers to those who graduated from high school or college in 1990s and early 2000s after the bursting of the bubble economy, and some, like Yamagami, have yet to find stable jobs and are now in their 40s and 50s.

During the period of their graduation, Japan was in the midst of decades-long economic slump, dubbed as the “lost 20 years”, and the labor market was worse than ever.

Between 1999 and 2005 more than 40% of newly college graduates were frozen out. The situation was even harder for high school graduates.

At that time most of the employment in Japan consisted of lifetime employment. As companies stuck to their traditional recruiting policy of hiring new graduates en masse even after the economy recovered, those who could not land a regular job remained left out and became low-paid, non-regular workers dim career prospects.

Their struggles continue to this day. "I had been working as a temp since I graduated from college in 2000, but I've been out of work for the past 2 years due to the pandemic," a 44-year-old woman in Tokyo said. "I live with my parents to cover my living expenses. But they are already in their mid-70s, and I cannot imagine how I can survive after they are none”.

”I could have been in the same situation as Yamagami, ” an anonymous social media account, appeared to be from the same generation as the suspect, posted after the assassination. "The crime he committed should never be justified. Having said that, how he suffered socially and economically is understandable. I feel the same pain of being left out.”

"Lost Generation" accounts for 20% of Japan's population. Although definitions vary, they are the children of the postwar Baby-Boomers born in the 1970s and early 1980s, and based on UN population estimates, approximately 20 million people fall into this category.

They are now in their 40s and early 50s, which, in Japanese corporate traditions, supposed to be prime working age.

There is a growing concern that the economic instability of Lost Generation would become an even more serious social problem in the years to come.

By 2050 they will be in their 70s and 80s, and the parents of the Baby Boomer generation who supported them will no longer be around. Although they would need social security benefits such as pensions and long-term care, some of them would be unable to qualify for those benefits because they could not make enough contribution during their working ages.

With Japan's declining birthrate and aging population, the burden on the generations below to support them would also increase.

According to an estimate published by Japan's Cabinet Secretariat in 2021, among people aged between 38 and 47, the number of NEETs - those who are not in employment, education or training- amounted to 390,000, and the number of unemployed people stood at 360,000, together account for 5% of the total of the generation.


There are as much as 3.6 million such non-regular workers of the generation, of whom more than 10%, or 400,000, are unwillingly working as temp because they are unable to find regular jobs.

Even if they are employed, prospects for retirement are still dim if they are working part time. According to a survey by the Ministry of Health, Labor and Welfare in Japan, non-full-time workers do not qualify for the various benefits and guarantees to which full-time employees are entitled. Fewer than one in six non-full-time workers receive retirement benefits, a major source of funds for retirement. The percentage of those eligible for employee pension insurance is less than 60% for non-full-time workers, compared to 96% for full-time workers.


In 2020, the Japanese government had set a target of increasing the number of full-time workers of "Lost Generation" by 300,000 over the three-year period from 2020 to 2022 and launched support measures, including job placement assistance for small businesses and agriculture in rural areas. However, partly due to the slump in the labor market during the pandemic, the actual increase was only 30,000, only one-tenth of the target, and the period was extended for two years.

The policies of the former Abe administration also included attempts to increase labor market flexibility and encourage labor participation by a variety of people, including women the elderly, and aslo mid-aged NEETs. However, the shocking assassination and the background of the suspect reminded people that more supports and changes should come.
